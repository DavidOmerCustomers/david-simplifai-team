Overview

Moving operational purchasing from manual process to controlled workflow

An internal purchasing and accounts payable process needed better visibility, fewer manual handoffs, and a cleaner way to coordinate purchase orders with outside vendors. I created a web-based purchasing and A/P system that supported internal request and approval workflows while giving vendors controlled access to manage their purchase orders and coordinate deliveries.

The project reflects the early-career pattern that continued throughout my work: translating operational friction into practical software, then designing the data and workflow structure so the system could be supported, reported on, and improved over time.

Problem

The process needed shared visibility and fewer handoffs

Purchasing and payables activity involved multiple participants, repeated status questions, document movement, vendor coordination, and manual follow-up. The risk was not just inefficiency; it was loss of visibility across the purchasing lifecycle.

  • Internal teams needed a clearer way to initiate and track purchasing activity.
  • Vendors needed controlled access to relevant purchase order information.
  • Delivery coordination required better status visibility across parties.
  • Accounts payable needed cleaner operational context for invoices, purchase orders, and fulfillment status.
  • Management needed reporting visibility without relying on scattered files or informal updates.
Approach

Design principle

Purchasing data should move through an explicit workflow, not disappear into email, phone calls, spreadsheets, or disconnected documents.

The system treated purchase orders, vendor updates, delivery coordination, and payables context as related workflow data. The goal was to create a shared operational record that reduced repeated follow-up and made the status of purchasing activity easier to understand.

Patterns

Core design patterns

Workflow-centered data model

Purchasing activity was modeled as a lifecycle with status, ownership, and related operational records rather than isolated documents.

Controlled vendor access

Vendors could access relevant purchase order information and coordinate delivery activity without exposing unrelated internal data.

PO and delivery visibility

Purchase orders and delivery coordination were connected so internal users could see operational status without repeated manual follow-up.

Accounts payable context

Payables activity could be tied back to purchasing and fulfillment context, improving traceability across the back-office process.

SQL-backed reporting

The system created structured data that could support operational reporting instead of relying on scattered files or tribal knowledge.

Business-first software design

The work started from real operational pain and translated business needs into a practical application that users could adopt.

Result

Result pattern

The resulting system gave purchasing, vendors, and accounts payable a clearer shared workflow around purchase orders and deliveries. It reduced fragmented coordination, improved operational visibility, and created a stronger data foundation for tracking purchasing activity.
